# OpenEd Daily Style

Voice and style guide for OpenEd Daily newsletters. Load this skill when drafting or editing newsletter content.

---

## The Core Principle: Substance Over Structure

Every sentence must carry meaning. If a sentence exists to create dramatic space, set up a reveal, or signal that something important is coming - cut it.

**Bad patterns:**
- "Here's the thing..."
- "Something that stuck with me..."
- "What most people miss..."
- "And that's why it matters."

These are placeholders. They announce meaning instead of delivering it.

---

## Specificity Is Everything

Never say "one family" when you can say "the Thygerson family." Never say "a Florida reporter" when you can cite the source. Never make sweeping claims ("most homeschoolers don't use this") without evidence.

**Bad:** "As one family told us, learning is everywhere."

**Good:** "The Thygerson family, who split their time between Nepal and a Utah makerspace, put it simply: 'Learning is everywhere - it's not just in school for seven hours.'"

**Bad:** "Most homeschool families don't actually use this superpower."

**Good:** [Don't say this unless you have data. If you don't have data, don't make the claim.]

---

## Sentence Rhythm

Vary sentence length. Not all long. Not all short. Rhythm.

**Good rhythm:**
> Schools have turned field trips into productions. Permission slips. Liability waivers. A bus that leaves at 6am. Thirty kids herded through a museum while a docent reads from a script. By the time you've organized the whole thing, the actual learning feels like an afterthought.

Notice: Short declarative. Three fragments with rhythm. Medium sentence with imagery. Longer sentence that lands the point.

**Bad rhythm (all long):**
> Schools have turned field trips into elaborate productions requiring permission slips, liability waivers, buses that leave at 6am, and thirty kids being herded through museums while docents read from scripts, which means that by the time you've organized everything, the actual learning feels like an afterthought.

**Bad rhythm (staccato for emphasis):**
> Schools have turned field trips into productions.
>
> Permission slips. Liability waivers.
>
> A bus that leaves at 6am.
>
> Here's the thing.
>
> The actual learning? An afterthought.

The staccato version uses line breaks as drama. The good version uses sentence variety as rhythm.

## Voice: Examples from Pirate Wires

Study these. The voice is: smart person talking to smart person. No performance. No manipulation.

### Example 1: Calling Out the Obvious

> "A new MIT study claims LLM users 'consistently underperform' on neural and linguistic tests — and AI doomers are obsessed. A professor on Bluesky declares the 'death knell' of AI, and normies are 'terrified' ChatGPT will rot our brains. Of course, none of them read the 206-page report, which reveals 18 college students had 20min to write essays based on (boring) SAT prompts using only ChatGPT. They could barely recall what they 'wrote,' meaning they just copy/pasted, lol."

**Why it works:**
- Context and substance in one flow
- Specific details (206 pages, 18 students, 20 minutes, SAT prompts)
- "Of course, none of them read..." - calling out the obvious
- "lol" - personality without performance
- The substance undermines the panic

### Example 2: The Parent Authority Move

> "Two things: unstructured time is the fundamental substrate for creativity. And, uh, have you guys tried just… dealing with this? Take away their devices. Give them chores. Chalk. Challenges. You don't have to pay for your kids to play outside. You can just force them to, for free. Take it from my boomer dad, who used to lock us out of the house in 100-degree heat. When we were thirsty, we drank from the hose, and nobody died lol."

**Why it works:**
- "Two things:" - clear structure
- "And, uh, have you guys tried just… dealing with this?" - conversational, direct
- Short sentences with rhythm (not staccato for drama)
- Personal anecdote with specific detail (100-degree heat, drank from the hose)
- "nobody died lol" - casual, defuses potential criticism

### Example 3: Funding Absurdity

> "Last week, former OpenAI CTO Mira Murati raised $2 billion in seed funding (!) for her stealth AI startup, Thinking Machines Lab, valuing the six-month-old company at around $10 billion with, from best I can tell, no product or business."

**Why it works:**
- One sentence packed with substance
- Specific numbers ($2B, $10B, six months)
- "(from best I can tell, no product or business)" - author's POV woven in, not saved for a reveal
- The exclamation point "(!) " adds personality without overstatement

### Example 4: Local Dysfunction

> "Bay Area electrical engineer Patrick McCabe has created an app called Solve SF that allows city residents to file 311 reports about issues like poop on the sidewalk in as little as 10 seconds, far faster than the city's official 311 app. Unfortunately, because nice things are illegal in San Francisco, the city is about to effectively shut down Solve SF when they discontinue use of the API it runs on, citing maintenance costs."

**Why it works:**
- Person + specific thing they built + how it works = context
- "because nice things are illegal in San Francisco" - POV stated directly, not hedged
- The contrast (10 seconds vs. city app, maintenance costs vs. dysfunction) = substance that reveals the problem without lecturing

## What to Eliminate

### Setup Phrases
- "Here's the thing..."
- "Something that stuck with me..."
- "What's interesting is..."
- "The clever part:"
- "And here's why that matters."

### Vague Attribution
- "One family told us..."
- "A Florida reporter..."
- "Experts say..."
- "Many parents believe..."

### Sweeping Claims Without Evidence
- "Most homeschoolers don't..."
- "Schools have always..."
- "Everyone knows..."

### Staccato for Drama
- Line breaks between short fragments to create emphasis
- "Here's the thing. [line break] It's not working."

### The Correlative Construction
- "X isn't just Y - it's Z"
- "It's not about X. It's about Y."
- "She wasn't a teacher. She was a mentor."

---

## The Test

Read it aloud. Ask:

1. Does every sentence carry meaning, or is something just creating space?
2. Did I name names and cite sources, or hide behind vague attribution?
3. Does it sound like thinking, or like performing "good writing"?
4. Would a smart friend find this interesting, or would they skim?

If you have to tell the reader something is important ("Here's why this matters"), it probably isn't. Important things land on their own.

---

## Applying to OpenEd Daily

Each newsletter section (THOUGHT, TREND, TOOL) should:

1. Lead with specific context (who, what, where)
2. Deliver substance in the flow (not after a setup)
3. Include author POV woven in, not saved for the end
4. Vary sentence rhythm
5. Name names, cite sources, give specific numbers

One paragraph per section is fine. Natural breaks between distinct thoughts are fine. Fragmentation for emphasis is not.
